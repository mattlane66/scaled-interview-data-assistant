# Mattress example walkthrough

This folder shows how to go from a raw interview transcript to a single-interview A2B artifact.

## Files in this example

- `transcript.md` — the raw interview
- `interview-card.md` — a worked single-interview A2B analysis
- `walkthrough.md` — a short explanation of how the transcript was interpreted

## Why this is a good starter example

This interview is useful because it contains all the key A2B ingredients in one place:

- a clear struggling moment
- a long period of workaround behavior
- visible push, pull, anxiety, and inertia
- a concrete tipping moment
- an adopted path
- a clear desired outcome that is not just a feature request

## How to read it correctly

### Point A is not “I need a new mattress.”

That is too broad.

The stronger Point A is the lived situation:

- he keeps waking up with headaches, neck pain, and back pain
- the mattress is visibly sinking
- he is trying to run a business, parent two small kids, and be less cranky at home
- the problem is happening repeatedly in a way that affects daily functioning

### Path Y is not Point A

Path Y includes what he did before switching:

- keep sleeping on the old mattress
- try sleeping without pillows
- adjust position
- flip and rotate the mattress
- take it off the frame and box springs
- investigate warranty options at Macy's
- delay action because life is crowded
- research online and nearly buy through Groupon

Those are workaround and substitute behaviors. They help explain the struggle, but they are not the struggling moment itself.

### The tipping moment is contextual

He did not go to Costco intending to buy a mattress.

The switch happened because several things lined up at once:

- he had already been dissatisfied for about a year
- he had done about three months of research
- he wanted memory foam but distrusted both mattress stores and risky online purchase paths
- he unexpectedly saw options in a trusted store five minutes from home
- the price felt manageable
- his wife quickly became supportive

That combination made the moment actionable.

### Point B is not “memory foam.”

Memory foam is a candidate path.

Point B is the progress he wanted:

- get a good night's sleep
- wake up feeling good enough to function
- be productive the next day
- be less cranky and more present as a dad and husband

## What this example teaches

1. The desired outcome should be stated as progress, not as a product.
2. Workarounds and complaints often appear before the true Point A is clearly stated.
3. Anxiety about the buying process can matter as much as dissatisfaction with the current solution.
4. Trusted context can be part of what makes a switch finally happen.
5. A good interview card should preserve uncertainty instead of pretending the transcript answered everything.

## Suggested way to use this example

Ask the assistant to:

1. read `examples/mattress/transcript.md`
2. produce its own interview card
3. compare the result against `examples/mattress/interview-card.md`
4. explain any differences in Point A, Path Y, tipping moment, Path X, or Point B

That makes this folder a concrete teaching and calibration artifact, not just a sample output.
